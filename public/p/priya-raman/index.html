How I think

The prompts say more than a résumé. ⭐ marks my starred answers.

★ My superpower at work
"Taking a vague, scary problem — 'our pipeline keeps falling over and nobody knows why' — and turning it into a sequenced plan a whole team can execute. I find the one bottleneck quietly causing eight downstream symptoms."
★ I'm proudest of
"Leading the re-architecture of our event pipeline from a monolith to event-driven services. The win wasn't elegant code — it was pulling it off with zero customer-facing downtime over six months while the business kept shipping on top of it."
★ The hardest thing I've shipped
"A multi-region failover for our payments service. The happy path was easy; the hard part was the dozen ways partial failures cascade. I spent more time on chaos tests and runbooks than on the feature — which is exactly why it held when we actually lost a region."
A mistake that taught me the most
"Early on I rewrote a 'messy' legacy service over a weekend, sure I understood it. I'd missed three edge cases the mess was quietly handling. Rough Monday. Now I treat old code as load-bearing until I can prove otherwise."
I'm the person people come to for
"Untangling production incidents calmly, and for honest design feedback. Teammates know I'll tell them if an approach will hurt in six months — kindly, but I won't rubber-stamp it."
Looking for a team that
"Treats reliability as a feature, not a chore — where 'it works on the happy path' isn't done. I do my best work with people who care about the boring 20% that keeps systems alive at 3am."
The skill I'm still building
"Saying no gracefully. I can scope and prioritize on paper, but I'm still learning to push back on a well-intentioned ask from a VP without caving or being too blunt."
I geek out about
"Database internals — query planners, write-ahead logs, how Postgres decides on an index. I'll happily spend an evening on a storage-engine paper, then bore my friends about it."
The kind of problems I want to solve next
"Scaling problems with real constraints — latency budgets, cost ceilings, correctness that can't bend. Less greenfield CRUD, more 'this has to handle 10x next year without falling over or bankrupting us.'"
What success looks like to me
"The systems I build get quietly boring — they just work and the team stops thinking about them. And the engineers I mentor outgrow me; if someone I coached makes a call I'd never have thought of, that's the win."
